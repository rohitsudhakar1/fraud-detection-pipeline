Why the model is shaped this way

At a 0.172% fraud rate, the naive model that predicts "never fraud" is 99.83% accurate and completely useless. Every design choice here fights that imbalance and the cost asymmetry behind it — a missed fraud is dollars out the door; a false positive is friction for a good customer.

01 / imbalance

SMOTE + class weighting

SMOTE synthesises minority samples in feature space; the boosters' class weighting reshapes the loss surface. Different fixes for the same problem — together they beat either alone.

02 / signal

XGB + LGBM ensemble

Two strong tabular learners with uncorrelated-enough errors. A 0.55 / 0.45 weighted blend of their calibrated probabilities lifts PR-AUC over either model on its own.

03 / trust

Isotonic calibration

Trees return uncalibrated scores. Isotonic regression on a held-out split makes score=0.8 actually mean "≈8 in 10 are fraud" — so downstream policy can trust the number.

04 / policy

PR-curve thresholding

The threshold is the F1-optimum on the precision-recall curve (t=0.55), not accuracy or ROC. Operating bands sit on top: review at 0.43, auto-block at 0.95.

Operational surface

The model is one piece. What makes it usable is the surface around it: low-latency serving, prediction logging for drift replay, and artifact versioning. Built to mirror how real fraud-risk teams operate.

Latency figures referenced in the architecture are design targets, to be measured against real CloudWatch percentiles once the Lambda is deployed. This is a portfolio project, not a production fraud system.
